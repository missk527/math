Notes (Key points from the speech)

What is active transport?
  • Active transport moves substances against their concentration gradient (from lower to higher concentration).
  • It requires energy (like pushing water up a waterfall) — energy mainly comes from cellular respiration (ATP).
  • It uses special membrane proteins (carriers/pumps) whose shapes are specific to the molecules transported.
Why not diffusion?
  • Diffusion is passive and only moves particles down a concentration gradient (high → low).
  • When the needed substance is already at a higher concentration inside the cell, diffusion cannot bring more in — active transport is required.
Example 1 — Root hair cells (plants)
  • Root hair cells have long projections that increase surface area for absorption from soil.
  • Some mineral ions (e.g., magnesium for chlorophyll) can be at a lower concentration in soil than inside the cell.
  • These ions are taken up by active transport, using energy from respiration → root hair cells contain many mitochondria.
Example 2 — Intestinal epithelial cells (animals)
  • After a meal, nutrient concentration is usually higher in the gut lumen than in blood, so nutrients can diffuse into the bloodstream at first.
  • As absorption continues, the diffusion gradient decreases. When diffusion alone is no longer effective, cells actively transport nutrients from the lumen into the cell, then they diffuse into the blood.
  • Intestinal cells have many tiny projections called microvilli (large surface area) and lots of mitochondria to supply energy.
Common adaptations for active transport
  • Large surface area (root hairs, microvilli) to increase uptake.
  • Many mitochondria to release energy by respiration.
  • Specific transport proteins in the membrane.
Active transport vs diffusion — quick compare
  • Direction: Diffusion down the gradient vs Active transport against the gradient.
  • Energy: Diffusion is passive (no energy) vs Active transport requires energy from respiration.
